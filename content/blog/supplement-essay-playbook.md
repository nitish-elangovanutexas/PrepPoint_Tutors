Supplements are where strong applicants accidentally become generic. The prompts look simple, but the **failure mode is always the same**: beautiful sentences with **no school-specific spine**. Use this playbook to stay specific without spending 40 hours per campus.

## The two layers every strong supplement has

1. **You layer**: a clear fact about your interests, values, or trajectory.
2. **School layer**: a concrete reason this school is a logical next step—not a prize you want to win.

If you remove the school name and the essay still makes sense, **you have not finished the school layer**.

## "Why us" in four beats (250–400 words)

Try this structure:

1. **Hook with a real detail** you discovered through research (a course sequence, professor line of work, club, lab, design studio, etc.).
2. **Bridge to your arc**: what you are building toward academically or personally.
3. **Name 2–3 resources** and explain **how you would use them** (not what they are famous for).
4. **Close with a forward-looking sentence** about what you want to contribute on campus.

Avoid Wikipedia facts (“great location,” “strong community”). Replace them with **mechanisms**: what you will do weekly, what you will try first term, what problem you want to explore.

## Activity essays: show decision-making, not a resume paragraph

If a prompt asks about an activity, the reader already saw the line on your activities list. Your job is to show:

- **What problem you were trying to solve** (even for a club or sport)
- **A moment you adapted** when something failed
- **What you would do differently** with more responsibility

One vivid scene beats five accomplishments.

## Revision checklist (5 minutes, high yield)

- **Name test**: swap school names—does it break? Good.
- **Verb check**: replace vague verbs (“learn,” “grow”) with **actions** (“build,” “test,” “organize,” “analyze”).
- **Cut prestige flattery**: admissions already knows they are selective.

---

> **Bottom line:** supplements reward specificity with evidence. Research like you mean it, then write like you already belong on campus.
