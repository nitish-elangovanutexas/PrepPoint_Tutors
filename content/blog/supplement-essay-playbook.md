Every cycle, admissions offices across the country read hundreds of thousands of essays. This cycle, a meaningful percentage of those essays will have been written — or heavily rewritten — by AI.

Admissions readers know this. Most of them have been doing this work for years, and they've adapted. The essays that stand out now are the ones that are unmistakably human — specific, honest, a little unpolished in the best way.

If you're using AI to write your college essays, you're not getting an edge. You're taking a risk that isn't worth it, and you're probably submitting something that sounds like everyone else.

Here's what's actually happening in admissions right now, and how to write an essay that works.

## How Admissions Officers Are Spotting AI Essays

I want to be honest with you: detection isn't perfect, and not every AI-generated essay gets flagged. But the essays that AI produces tend to share certain qualities that experienced readers notice — even if they can't always name them.

**They're smooth in a way real student writing isn't.** Real 17-year-old writing has rhythm, idiosyncrasy, awkward moments that get corrected and slightly different awkward moments that don't. AI prose is consistently polished in a way that feels uncanny over 650 words.

**They're general where they should be specific.** AI tends to make broad, uplifting statements about growth and resilience and the importance of community. Strong college essays make specific statements about a specific afternoon, a specific person, a specific realization. The difference is enormous.

**They use certain constructions that AI overuses.** Phrases like "I came to realize," "this experience taught me," and "as I reflect on" appear at a rate in AI-generated essays that borders on statistical. Readers notice.

**They don't sound like the rest of the application.** When an essay reads like it was written by a professional writer but the short-answer responses and activity descriptions sound like a high schooler, that inconsistency raises questions.

Beyond detection, there's a deeper problem: even when AI essays aren't flagged, they usually aren't compelling. They're competent. They say the right things. They don't give readers a reason to advocate for you.

## What Admissions Officers Are Actually Looking For

The college essay has one job: to make the admissions reader feel like they know you.

Not impressed by you. Not overwhelmed by your accomplishments. Know you — as a person, with a specific way of seeing things and a particular place in the world.

This is why so many students make the mistake of writing about their biggest achievement, their hardest challenge, or their most exotic experience. These topics aren't bad. They're just hard to pull off, because they tempt you toward abstraction. You end up writing about how hard the experience was, what you learned, and how you grew — when what the reader wants is to see *you* in a specific moment, thinking specific thoughts, noticing specific things.

The essays that work best are often about something small. A running joke with a sibling. The way your grandmother makes tea. The ritual of warming up before a game. Small subjects done with precision and genuine feeling are almost always more effective than large subjects done with vague gestures toward meaning.

## How to Use AI as a Tool (Without Letting It Write Your Essay)

I use AI with my students all the time. The key is treating it as a thinking partner, not a ghostwriter.

**Use AI to surface your own ideas.** Ask it to interview you. Literally: "Ask me ten questions that might help me figure out what to write my college essay about." Answer them honestly. The answers are yours. The essay starts there.

**Use AI to diagnose problems, not to fix them.** Once you have a draft, paste it in and ask: "What parts of this essay feel vague or generic? What specific details are missing? What questions does this leave unanswered?" Use that feedback to go back and rewrite — yourself.

**Use AI to check structure, not voice.** You can ask whether the essay has a clear arc, whether the opening is engaging, whether the ending lands. You can't ask it to make the writing better without it taking over the voice.

The test: if you read the essay back and it sounds like you — really you, the way you actually talk and think — you're in good shape. If it sounds like a well-meaning adult wrote something about you, start over.

## The Supplement Essays: Where Most Students Lose Points They Don't Realize They Had

The main Common App essay gets a lot of attention. The supplement essays — the shorter, school-specific ones — often get treated as an afterthought.

This is a mistake.

For schools that ask "Why [School]?" — and most competitive schools do — this essay is your opportunity to demonstrate that you've done real research and have a genuine reason for wanting to be there. A generic "why" essay that talks about the school's reputation and vibrant campus community is a waste of the prompt. Readers have seen it ten thousand times.

A strong "why" essay names specific programs, specific professors, specific opportunities that connect directly to your specific interests. It shows that you've thought about this school as a place where your particular version of intellectual curiosity or professional interest fits.

For schools that ask about your intended major, the goal is to show the origin of the interest — not to prove you've already mastered the subject. How did you first get interested in this? What question are you still trying to answer? What experience, class, book, or conversation pushed you further in?

For personal insight questions (UC essays, UT essays) — each prompt is a different angle on the same core question: who are you, and what matters to you? Answer these directly. Don't try to be clever. Be honest.

## A Note on Authenticity

I work with students on their essays every cycle, and the moments where the writing really clicks are always the same: it's when a student stops trying to write something impressive and just tells me what actually happened.

That's the essay. Not the polished version of it — the honest, specific, particular version.

AI can't write that, because AI doesn't know what happened. Only you do.
